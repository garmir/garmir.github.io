Reconnecting Through Community and Technological Decentralization

In 2021, a post titled “Dead Internet Theory: Most Of The Internet Is Fake” was published by a user named “IlluminatiPirate” on the forum Agora Road’s Macintosh Cafe. The post claimed that, due to coordinated and intentional efforts, the Internet since around 2016 or 2017 has consisted mainly of bot activity and automatically generated content—manipulated by algorithmic curation to control the population and minimize genuine human input. While this claim is obviously subjective and veers into the realm of conspiracy, it’s also glaringly apparent that the current online landscape—and the technology powering it—is no longer representative of the broader public’s best interests. Social media’s unrelenting onslaught of information has contributed to a society that is horrifically divided, antagonistic, and deeply detrimental to itself and the environment around it. Individuals have been drawn into polarizing themselves based on political ideology, class, gender, and race, among countless other factors. Of course, identity and individuality are irrefutably important, but when our focus on these aspects hinders our ability to organize, empathize, and form communities, it distracts us from actionably holding accountable those who are truly detrimental to society.

The current global political climate is similarly bleak, and I, like many others, find it hard to have much hope for the future. Infighting grounded in supposed political alignment is neither productive nor conducive to meaningful progress. It feels as though we’ve shifted away from a moral and ethical approach to life, and instead label ourselves based on an oversimplified “Left versus Right” spectrum. Historically, democratic systems have had relative success compared to other governmental structures, but it’s increasingly clear that we no longer have a genuine voice in the governments that purport to represent us. Standards of living have rapidly declined; imperialistic regimes and proxy wars continue unabated despite widespread protest, driven by self-imposed global “leaders” interested only in broadening their already unquantifiable wealth. The largest Western superpower operates as a capitalistic oligarchy—founded through slavery and genocide—and its leadership is effectively determined by the richest individuals on Earth, including but not limited to a vocal and unapologetic Nazi. Corporations monetize fundamental human necessities like food, water, and healthcare, offering no real benefit or service apart from limiting supply to feed their own greed, while broken property laws allow landlords to profit indefinitely off the underprivileged. And I could continue on…

So what hope do we have for improvement? It’s all too easy to become numb and apathetic in the face of perpetual chaos, especially when we’re inundated with social media updates and news alerts that highlight global dysfunction—often leaving us feeling powerless to enact any meaningful change. But despite the cynicism that surrounds modern technology and industry, there remain tangible ways we can restructure our relationship with the internet and the tools we use every day. By focusing on benefiting individuals, building genuine community, and freely sharing knowledge, we can begin to undo the damage caused by relentless privatization, commercialization, and a lack of environmental sustainability.

In the face of widespread corporate control and political corruption, it’s understandable to view technology as yet another means of surveillance, division, and manipulation. But technology itself isn’t inherently problematic—it’s how it’s deployed, who controls it, and what ends it’s engineered to serve that truly matter. By reorienting our use of technology around principles of autonomy, community resilience, and mutual aid, we can repurpose these same tools to help us organize effectively and compassionately, rather than being cogs in an unfeeling capitalist machine.

Right now, the internet—once imagined as a free and open landscape for sharing information—has been twisted into a system that surveils us, harvests our data, sells our attention, and mediates our relationships through platforms owned by a handful of billionaires. Social media, once promising for genuine connection, has devolved into endless outrage cycles and trivial distractions, driving us further apart and away from real-world action. Yet we don’t have to settle for this status quo. There are concrete steps we can take to reclaim our digital spaces, prioritize privacy and sustainability, and foster human connection over profit and control.

Recognizing Our Reliance on Extractive Platforms

A critical first move is admitting that we rely heavily on platforms that simply don’t have our best interests at heart. Companies like Meta (Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp), Google, and TikTok operate on the logic of surveillance capitalism—monetizing our every interaction, selling our personal data, and quietly shaping our worldview through opaque algorithms.

Thankfully, there are decentralized, privacy-respecting, and community-centric alternatives that give us more control over our online presence. However, not every service marketed as “private” or “secure” stands up to scrutiny. Two examples often cited are Signal (messaging) and DuckDuckGo (search engine), each with notable downsides to be aware of.

  • Signal (Messaging App)
    Pros: Uses end-to-end encryption and stores minimal metadata.
    Cons: Requires a phone number for registration, creating a link between identity and usage. It uses centralized servers, so there’s still a measure of trust involved.
    Possible Alternative: Session (no phone number required, onion-routed for anonymity) or self-hosted XMPP servers with OMEMO encryption.
  • DuckDuckGo (Search Engine)
    Pros: Positioned as privacy-friendly, doesn’t log typical user data the way Google does.
    Cons: Relies heavily on Microsoft’s Bing index, and evidence suggests DuckDuckGo whitelists certain Microsoft trackers. Lacks robust transparency and doesn’t provide a self-hosting option.
    Possible Alternatives: A self-hosted SearxNG instance for maximum control, or Brave Search, which uses its own index.

Self-Hosting & Community-Run Infrastructure

Depending on cloud services and corporate hosting involves surrendering control of our data to companies that profit from it. Self-hosting is an empowering alternative—but it comes with the responsibility of maintaining security, bearing costs, and sometimes relying on hostile internet service providers (ISPs).

  • Security: Misconfiguration can expose sensitive information; regular updates and encryption are non-negotiable.
  • ISP Dependence: Even hosting at home still involves centralized infrastructure that can be throttled or monitored.
  • Cost & Maintenance: Purchasing hardware, paying for electricity and bandwidth, and ongoing upkeep demands both financial resources and technical know-how.

Despite these hurdles, self-hosting can grant greater privacy, control, and resilience against censorship.

Some Self-Hosted Tools & Alternatives:

  • File Storage & Sync
    Nextcloud: Feature-rich Google Drive alternative (file sharing, calendar, contacts). Resource-intensive but powerful.
    Syncthing: Peer-to-peer file syncing with no central server; simpler, but no browser-based file management.
  • Email Hosting
    Mail-in-a-Box: Straightforward way to set up your own mail server. Requires spam-blocking vigilance.
    ProtonMail Bridge: Encrypts mail via ProtonMail while letting you use familiar apps like Thunderbird. Not fully self-hosted, but a step up from Gmail.
  • Web Hosting
    WriteFreely: Federated, minimalist blogging platform that integrates with the Fediverse (Mastodon).
    Ghost: Modern publishing platform for independent blogging and newsletters.
    Discourse: Community forum software well-suited to fostering collaboration.

If fully self-hosting isn’t achievable—maybe due to costs, maintenance, or limited technical skills—another option is to support community-run hosting initiatives, which operate under more ethical and transparent models than Big Tech giants.

Mesh Networks for Decentralized Internet Access

For many marginalized communities, reliable internet access isn’t guaranteed. Even in better-connected areas, corporate or government control can disrupt or restrict access. Mesh networks offer a decentralized, community-oriented approach by interlinking routers or nodes to create an autonomous, resilient network that doesn’t rely solely on a single ISP or authority.

  • Tools like LibreMesh or OpenWrt can help set up mesh networks using affordable, off-the-shelf routers.
  • Urban groups such as NYC Mesh have demonstrated how to deliver affordable, fast service outside traditional ISP monopolies.
  • Rural areas and regions with limited infrastructure can also benefit, using mesh networks to share resources and maintain connectivity during crises or political upheavals.

Ethics & Mutual Aid

Ultimately, reclaiming technology isn’t just about swapping apps or servers—it’s about ethics. Open-source projects, decentralized platforms, and self-hosting efforts thrive on values like cooperation, transparency, and collective ownership. They rely on mutual aid, whether through contributing code, translating documentation, offering technical support, or simply spreading the word.

By recognizing how our digital practices either feed into or push against commercialization and surveillance, we can shift toward more autonomous, community-driven online spaces. Technology doesn’t have to be a tool that undermines our privacy or isolates us from one another. When we collectively demand better and support alternatives that reflect our values, we help create a digital ecosystem centered on genuine human connection and shared well-being, rather than perpetual division and profit extraction.

It won’t happen overnight, and it won’t be easy. But if we take actionable steps—switching to decentralized platforms, supporting local and community-driven projects, learning to self-host or contributing to open-source efforts—we stand a much better chance of fostering a healthier online (and offline) future. We can begin to reclaim not only our relationship with technology, but also our sense of collective agency and hope.
